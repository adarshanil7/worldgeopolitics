1947

The Great Divide

"A moment comes, which comes but rarely in history, when we step out from the old to the new."

I. The Exit II. The Radcliffe Line III. The Bloodbath IV. Kashmir

It was the end of the British Empire and the beginning of a tragedy. Bankrupt after WWII, Britain rushed to leave India. The question was: How to leave?

Religious tensions between Hindus and Muslims were boiling. Lord Mountbatten, the last Viceroy, decided the only solution was to cut the country in two: Secular India and Muslim Pakistan.

II. The Radcliffe Line

"He had never been to India before. He was given five weeks to draw the border."

A British lawyer named Cyril Radcliffe was tasked with drawing the border. He knew nothing of the geography or culture. Using outdated maps, he drew a line that bisected villages, rivers, and families. The map was kept secret until *after* independence.

III. The Largest Migration in History

When the line was revealed, panic erupted. 15 million people fled—Hindus to India, Muslims to Pakistan. Trains were ambush targets, arriving at stations filled with corpses. Up to 2 million people died in the communal violence.

IV. The Legacy of Kashmir

The Princely State of Kashmir was left in limbo. Its Hindu ruler joined India, but its majority Muslim population wavered. This triggered the first of four wars between India and Pakistan. Today, it remains one of the most militarized zones on earth—a nuclear flashpoint.
