1947 — 1991

The Iron Curtain

"From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, an iron curtain has descended across the Continent." — Churchill

I. Two Worlds II. Containment III. Proxy Wars IV. The Collapse

KEY FIGURES

  • Harry S. Truman
  • Joseph Stalin

The guns of World War II had barely fallen silent when a new, quieter war began. It was not fought with armies on a battlefield, but with spies, diplomacy, and the threat of nuclear annihilation.

The world split into two armed camps: the Capitalist West (led by the USA) and the Communist East (led by the USSR). What followed was a 45-year standoff that defined the modern world.

II. The Long Telegram

ARCHIVE: STATE DEPT

"The Soviet power... is impervious to logic of reason, and it is highly sensitive to logic of force."

— George Kennan, 1946

In 1946, a US diplomat named George Kennan sent an 8,000-word telegram from Moscow. His thesis changed history: The USSR was inherently expansionist and could not be reasoned with. The only strategy was Containment—using economic and military pressure to stop the spread of Communism until the Soviet system fell apart from its own internal contradictions.

III. Fighting in the Shadows

Because both sides possessed nuclear weapons, a direct war meant mutual suicide (MAD). So they fought Proxy Wars. In Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan, and Angola, the superpowers funded local armies to do the dying for them.

NATO

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization. "Keep the Russians out, the Americans in, and the Germans down."

Warsaw Pact

The Soviet response. A military alliance of Eastern Bloc satellite states controlled by Moscow.

IV. The Collapse

Kennan was right. The Soviet economy, burdened by military spending and lack of innovation, began to rot. When Mikhail Gorbachev tried to reform it (Glasnost/Perestroika) in the 1980s, the whole structure crumbled. In 1989, the Berlin Wall fell. In 1991, the USSR ceased to exist. The West had won without firing a shot in Europe.
